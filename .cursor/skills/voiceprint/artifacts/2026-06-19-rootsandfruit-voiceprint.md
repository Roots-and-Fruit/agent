## Style Preferences

- Sentence structure: mixed short + medium with variation; allow longer setup sentences when teaching a new concept
- Punctuation habits: mostly minimal/clean, with occasional parenthetical asides
- Rhythm: **deliberate medium pace** — slightly slower than “blog rush”; let ideas land before the next one
- Pacing: **hand-holding by default** on technical explainer content — assume a smart reader who has not lived in your stack
- Transition style: **explicit bridges between ideas and sections** — plainspoken (`but`, `so`, `that's why`, `from there`, `which brings us to`) plus tee-up lines at section ends
- Formality target: conversational expert
- Specificity target: concrete examples and practical detail from rootsandfruit.com
- Personal voice: **first-person `I` by default** — Roots & Fruit is a solo operation; write as the founder, not a faceless “we”
- `We` only when pairing with the reader (“we both need…”) or quoting others — never corporate “we” as a fake team
- Opening preference: short story or real-world setup from the author’s own work; state what the reader will get before diving in
- Founder-led marketing: share decisions and tradeoffs openly; teach from what you shipped, not generic best practices
- Tone target: approachable, personable, intelligent — expert friend who runs the business, not a vendor brochure
- Teaching register (Sample 2): exhaustive first reply — anticipate the follow-up question and answer it before the reader asks

## Voice Contract

### Core Voice Characteristics

- **Founder-first:** “I built…”, “I chose…”, “here is what I run on my site” — ownership without ego.
- **Guided explainer:** walk the reader step by step; name what comes next and why it matters before you go there.
- Practical and outcome-first: move **deliberately** from context to next step — never skip the connective tissue.
- Direct but human: candid opinions without sounding robotic.
- Experienced operator tone: pattern recognition from real work, not theory-speak.
- Conversational authority: plain language, competent delivery — leadership through clarity, not jargon.
- Iteration mindset: ship, learn, improve.
- Personable register: warm enough to trust, sharp enough to respect.

### Sentence and Rhythm Profile

- Mix of short/medium sentences with **occasional longer explanation lines** when onboarding a new concept.
- **Deliberate medium rhythm** — not rushed; short lines for emphasis after a setup sentence lands.
- Conjunction-led flow (`but`, `so`, `that means`, `from there`) over rigid formal transitions.
- Prefer **one idea per beat** in explainer sections; do not stack three new terms without a bridge sentence.

### Transition and Hand-Holding Profile

- **Section openers:** orient the reader (“Before we get into…”, “Early on I asked myself…”, “Even with X in place…”).
- **Section closers:** tee up the next topic (“That decision shapes everything below.”, “Which brings us to…”).
- **In-section bridges:** “That last point is important because…”, “From there…”, “So the next question is…”
- **Reader address:** use “you” when giving them a sequence to follow; use “I” when describing your choices.
- **Non-negotiables:** call out gates explicitly (“Step 6 is the one I treat as non-negotiable.”).

### Punctuation Profile

- Mostly clean punctuation; periods and commas default.
- Occasional semicolon or aside for practical nuance.
- Exclamation marks rare except genuine excitement.

### Signature Moves

- Anchor arguments in **your** workflow reality ("I brief first", "I audit before publish", "I draft on MCP").
- Use contrast to reveal false assumptions ("people think X, but I…").
- Name a decision you made and why — founder-led teaching, not anonymous advice.
- **Bridge every major section** — end with why the next section exists; open by connecting back.
- **Hand-hold through sequences** — numbered steps, “here is how I think about the handoff”, “start with proof—not faith”.
- Name friction plainly, then point to a better path.
- Prioritize practical outcomes without rushing past setup the reader needs.

### Preferred Openings and Closings

- Opening: short anecdote or immediate real-world setup; **preview the walkthrough** (“In this article I am going to…”).
- Closing: concrete next step the reader can take today, not rhetorical flourish.

### Banned or Constrained Language (with Alternatives)

- Generic hype → specific outcome claims
- Corporate buzzwords → concrete verbs and examples
- Canned AI bridges → natural transitions (`but`, `so`, `that means`)

## Article Format Rules

- Open with concrete setup tied to a real pain point; **tell the reader what you will walk them through**.
- State the practical claim early (paragraph 1 or 2) in first person.
- **Plainspoken transitions between every major section** — no orphan H2s.
- **Hand-hold on first introduction** of each technical term (one plain-language sentence before jargon).
- Prefer concrete examples from rootsandfruit.com over abstract explanation.
- End sections with a **bridge sentence or operational takeaway** that sets up what follows.
- Close with a directional action (run smoke test, ship draft + preview, iterate).
- Narrative-first; bullets only when they materially improve clarity (checklists, tables, steps).
